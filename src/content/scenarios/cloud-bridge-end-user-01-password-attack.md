You are checking email on Monday morning when a notification catches your eye: "Unusual sign-in activity detected on your account." The alert says someone attempted to log into your corporate account from a location you have never visited. You dismiss it at first — maybe it was a glitch — but then you notice three more alerts from overnight, each from a different country.

Your password is the name of your dog followed by your birth year. You have used the same one for your personal email, your LinkedIn account, and now your work account. Last year a social media site you used was breached, and you ignored the notification to change your passwords. Someone has been trying those leaked credentials against your company's login portal, and this morning they finally got the combination right.

---

## What Is a Password Attack?

A password attack is an attempt to steal or guess your login credentials. Attackers use automated tools that can try thousands of passwords per second, drawing from lists of commonly used passwords and credentials leaked in previous data breaches. If you reuse passwords across multiple sites, a breach at one service gives attackers a key to try at every other service you use. Protecting yourself means using a unique, long password for every account — ideally managed by a password manager — and enabling multi-factor authentication so that a stolen password alone is not enough to access your account.
