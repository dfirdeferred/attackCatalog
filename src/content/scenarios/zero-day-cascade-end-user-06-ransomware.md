You arrive at work to find your colleagues standing in the parking lot. The office is open, but every computer screen displays the same message: a red and black page demanding payment in Bitcoin to unlock the company's files. The phone systems are down. The badge readers are offline. Someone propped the doors open with a chair.

Your manager gathers the team in a conference room lit by cell phone flashlights — the smart lighting system runs on the network too. She explains that every digital system the company operates has been encrypted by ransomware. Email, file servers, the customer database, payroll — all of it locked. The attackers are demanding millions.

You think about the presentation you spent three weeks preparing, the client proposals in your inbox, the personal photos you saved to your desktop because your phone was full. All of it is gone unless the company pays or IT can somehow recover from backups. People around you are whispering about layoffs if the company cannot recover.

## What Is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software that locks all the files on a computer or network by encrypting them — scrambling the data so it cannot be read without a special digital key. The attackers then demand payment, usually in cryptocurrency like Bitcoin, in exchange for the key to unlock the files. Modern ransomware attacks often target entire organizations at once, locking every computer simultaneously. Some attackers also steal copies of the data first and threaten to publish it publicly if the ransom is not paid, creating a double threat of both data loss and data exposure.
