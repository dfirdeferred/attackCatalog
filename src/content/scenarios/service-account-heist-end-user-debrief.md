The Service Account Heist affected every employee in the organization. What began as a technical compromise of automated service accounts cascaded into a breach that exposed every person's credentials. You experienced it as suspicious emails, mandatory password changes, application outages, system restrictions, and ultimately a company-wide credential reset.

Your role in the incident was not passive. The social engineering email you received in chapter one was part of the attacker's reconnaissance. The password you changed helped contain the damage. The applications you avoided using during the lockdown gave IT the space they needed to respond. Every action mattered.

---

## Lessons Learned

Even when the technical details are invisible to you, your actions directly impact the organization's security posture. Key takeaways: be suspicious of unusual requests for information about your team, applications, or workflows — even if they appear to come from IT, use a unique strong password for every account and manage them with a password manager so that a breach at work does not compromise your personal life, cooperate promptly with IT security directives during incidents because speed matters, report anything that seems unusual rather than assuming it is a false alarm, and understand that application outages during security incidents are protective measures — IT is closing doors that the attacker could use. Your awareness and cooperation are essential components of organizational defense.
